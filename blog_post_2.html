What Do You Do When a Client Doesn't Pay Their Overdue Invoice?

January 21, 2026 CDEF Editorial Team 8 min read No Comments

You finished the task flawlessly. The client is satisfied. You've invoiced on time. And then… silence. Days turn to weeks, weeks to months, and that invoice balance stays stubbornly unpaid. This scenario plays out thousands of times daily across Indian businesses. So what do you actually do when a client simply won't pay?

According to industry data, over 40% of B2B invoices in India are paid late, and approximately 15% go unpaid entirely without proactive follow-up. The solution lies in having a clear, escalating action plan.

Step 1: Verify Before You Escalate

Before assuming bad intent, confirm the invoice was received and is not stuck in a bureaucratic approvals queue. Send a polite confirmation email asking whether the invoice was received and if there are any queries. Sometimes invoices are lost, disputed, or simply deprioritised — a gentle nudge resolves more cases than you'd expect.

Also verify: Is the invoice addressed to the correct entity? Are the payment details accurate? Does it reference the correct purchase order number? Errors here give clients legitimate reasons to delay processing.

The Escalation Timeline That Works

  • Day 1 Past Due — Friendly ReminderSend a polite email with the invoice attached. Reference the due date and offer to clarify any queries. Keep tone warm and professional.
  • Day 7 — Follow-Up CallA direct phone call from accounts or a relationship manager. Confirm receipt and ask for an expected payment date. Document the conversation.
  • Day 14 — Formal NoticeSend a formal written notice on company letterhead stating the overdue amount and a deadline (typically 7 days) for payment before further action.
  • Day 30 — Escalate to Senior ManagementEngage your own CFO or MD to contact the client's financial decision-maker directly. This signals seriousness without burning the relationship.
  • Day 45+ — Engage a Recovery SpecialistProfessional debt recovery agencies like CDEF have the tools, legal knowledge, and negotiation skills to recover dues where internal efforts have failed.

How to Communicate Without Burning Bridges

The fear of damaging a business relationship often paralyses companies from pursuing overdue payments. But consider this: a client who consistently pays late is already damaging the relationship — they're just doing it quietly. Your right to payment is not a request; it's a contractual obligation the client agreed to.

Language matters enormously. There's a significant difference between "You owe us money and it's overdue" and "We wanted to follow up on Invoice #4521 which became due on the 5th. Please let us know if there's anything we can assist with to facilitate processing." Both communicate the same thing; only one preserves the relationship.

Communication Template

Subject: Invoice #[NUMBER] — Payment Due [DATE] | [YOUR COMPANY NAME]. "Dear [Name], I hope this finds you well. This is a gentle follow-up regarding Invoice #[NUMBER] for ₹[AMOUNT], which became due on [DATE]. Please let us know if you require any documentation to process payment, or if there are any queries we can address. We value our partnership and look forward to your continued business."

When to Involve Legal Channels

If 60–90 days have elapsed without payment and communication has broken down, legal options become relevant. In India, businesses can pursue recovery through:

  • MSME Samadhaan Portal — for MSMEs dealing with buyers who delay payment beyond 45 days
  • Civil Suit for Recovery of Money — under the Civil Procedure Code for documented dues
  • Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act — if a cheque has bounced
  • Arbitration — if your contract contains an arbitration clause
  • Insolvency proceedings — for very large dues where the debtor may be insolvent

Legal action is time-consuming and costly, which is why engaging a professional recovery agency before reaching this stage almost always yields better outcomes — faster recovery, lower cost, and preserved relationships.

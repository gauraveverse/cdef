Legal Steps to Take When an Invoice Goes Unpaid

September 11, 2025 CDEF Editorial Team 8 min read No Comments

You've sent the invoice. You've sent three reminders. You've made six follow-up calls. And still — nothing. The payment that was due 90 days ago sits unreceived while your own suppliers and staff wait to be paid.

At this point, informal collection has run its course. It's time to understand the legal mechanisms available to you — and use them strategically. India's legal framework provides multiple escalation pathways for recovering unpaid commercial debts, each suited to different debt sizes, debtor types, and timelines.

Important note: Legal action should be a structured last resort, not a knee-jerk reaction. The steps in this guide are designed to maximise recovery while minimising cost and relationship damage where possible.

Before You Go Legal: The Pre-Action Checklist

Before initiating any formal legal proceedings, ensure you have completed these critical steps. Courts and tribunals look favourably on creditors who demonstrate they attempted reasonable resolution first.

  1. Document everything: Gather all invoices, delivery receipts, work completion certificates, email correspondence, and payment records. A weak paper trail is the most common reason legal actions fail.
  2. Send a formal demand notice: A written demand on company letterhead or via a lawyer, giving the debtor a fixed deadline (typically 15–30 days) to pay or respond. This is a prerequisite for most legal proceedings.
  3. Attempt structured negotiation: Before filing anything, offer the debtor a documented payment plan. A partial recovery today often beats a full recovery after 18 months of litigation.
  4. Calculate your total claim: Include the principal invoice amount, any contractually agreed late payment interest, and out-of-pocket recovery costs you can substantiate.

Legal Pathway 1: MSME Samadhaan (For MSME Creditors)

If your business is registered as an MSME under the MSMED Act 2006, the MSME Samadhaan portal is your fastest and most cost-effective legal tool. This government-run conciliation mechanism handles disputes between MSMEs and their buyers, with a statutory mandate to resolve cases within 90 days.

How it works: You file an application online at samadhaan.msme.gov.in with your invoice details, demand notice evidence, and basic company information. The buyer is summoned to the facilitation council. If they don't appear or fail to pay, the council issues an arbitral award that is enforceable like a court decree.

Best for: Debts owed to MSME creditors by larger buyers; particularly effective when the debtor is a listed company or a PSU that is sensitive to regulatory notices.

MSME Samadhaan Success Rate

Cases filed by registered MSMEs against larger corporate buyers have a settlement rate exceeding 60% within the conciliation phase — before the formal arbitration award is even issued. The threat of a conciliation notice alone often triggers payment.

Legal Pathway 2: Section 138, Negotiable Instruments Act (Cheque Bounce)

If your debtor issued a post-dated cheque that was dishonoured upon presentation, Section 138 of the NI Act provides one of the most powerful recovery tools available. This is a criminal provision — meaning the debtor faces potential imprisonment of up to 2 years plus a fine of twice the cheque amount.

Key requirements: The cheque must have bounced for insufficient funds (not stopped payment or technical issues), you must have sent a formal demand notice within 30 days of receiving the bank dishonour memo, and you must file the complaint within 30 days of the demand notice deadline.

Strict timelines apply: Missing the Section 138 filing window permanently bars this remedy. If you have a dishonoured cheque, consult a lawyer immediately — do not delay.

Legal Pathway 3: Civil Recovery Suit (Order XXXVII – Summary Suit)

For undisputed commercial debts based on written contracts, invoices, or promissory notes, Order XXXVII of the CPC allows you to file a summary suit — a faster civil proceedings track that doesn't require full trial procedures unless the defendant can show a triable issue of fact.

How it works: File in the appropriate civil court based on jurisdiction (district court for smaller amounts, high court for larger claims). The defendant has limited time to respond and must seek leave to defend. In many cases, courts grant interim orders or conditional leave to defend, essentially forcing partial payment upfront.

Jurisdiction and filing fees: Civil courts in India typically charge filing fees as a percentage of claim value, ranging from 0.5% to 7.5% depending on state. This cost should factor into your decision for smaller debts.

Legal Pathway 4: Arbitration (If Your Contract Has an Arbitration Clause)

If your service agreement or purchase order contains an arbitration clause, you must use arbitration rather than courts. While traditionally slower than courts, institutional arbitration through bodies like ICADR or FICCI ARB has become significantly faster — with many commercial disputes resolved in 12–18 months.

Arbitration awards are enforceable under the Arbitration & Conciliation Act 1996 and carry the same weight as court decrees. International counterparties often prefer arbitration due to neutrality and confidentiality.

Legal Pathway 5: IBC – Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (For Large Debts)

For debts exceeding ₹1 crore, the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016 provides a powerful tool: filing an Operational Creditor petition before the National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT). Once admitted, the NCLT initiates the Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP), essentially threatening the debtor's continued operations.

The mere act of issuing a Section 8 demand notice under the IBC (which precedes the NCLT filing) has proven extraordinarily effective — many large debtors pay in full within the 10-day response window to avoid NCLT proceedings being initiated against them.

IBC Demand Notice: A Powerful Pre-Litigation Tool

Even if you never intend to actually file at NCLT, the Section 8 IBC demand notice sent by a lawyer carries enormous weight. It signals seriousness, triggers a statutory 10-day response deadline, and is often enough to resolve debts that months of informal chasing could not.

Choosing the Right Legal Path: A Decision Framework

The right legal channel depends on four factors: the debt amount, your registration status, the debtor's profile, and the quality of your documentation.

  • Debt under ₹20 lakhs + MSME creditor: MSME Samadhaan — fastest and free
  • Dishonoured cheque: Section 138 NI Act immediately
  • Debt ₹5–₹50 lakhs, clear documentation: Summary civil suit (Order XXXVII)
  • Arbitration clause in contract: Institutional arbitration proceedings
  • Debt over ₹1 crore, corporate debtor: IBC Section 8 notice followed by NCLT if needed
  • Multiple debtors, ongoing recovery programme: Engage a professional collection agency like CDEF
